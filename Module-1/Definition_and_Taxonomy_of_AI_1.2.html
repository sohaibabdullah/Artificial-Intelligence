Definition and Taxonomy of AI

What is AI

Artificial Intelligence (AI) is about THINKING. But it's not only about thinking it's also about PERCEPTION and it's also about ACTION.

Artificial Intelligence is about building models that are targeted at thinking, perception and action.

But in order to have a model, you have to have representations. So we can say, AI is about representations that support the making of models to facilitate an understanding of thinking, perception and action.

Representations make constraints of a problem exposed and that's why we build representations [Example: Farmer, Fox, Chicken and Grain game]. That's why we do algebra in high school because algebraic notation exposes the constraints that make it possible to figure out how many customers we get for the number of advertisements we place in the newspapers.

AI is also about algorithms (procedures or methods) because in the end we need to write programs. So we can say:

Artificial intelligence is about algorithms enabled by constraints exposed by representations that support models targeted at thinking, perception, and action.

[Ref: Patrick Henry Winston, professor at MIT]

Level of Intelligence

In terms of level of intelligence AI can be classified into: Artificial Narrow Intelligence (ANI), Artificial General Intelligence (AGI) and Artificial Super Intelligence (ASI).

1. ANI: Artificial Narrow Intelligence (or "Weak AI")

Definition: AI designed to perform a specific task or a narrow set of tasks. It operates within a pre-defined range or context.

Capability Level: It equals or surpasses human capabilities, but only in that specific domain. It cannot transfer its learning to entirely unrelated tasks.

Current Status: All AI that exists today is ANI.

Examples: A chess-playing algorithm that can beat a grandmaster but cannot play checkers; autonomous driving systems; facial recognition software; and even advanced systems like ChatGPT and Midjourney. Even though ChatGPT seems highly capable, it is still "narrowly" predicting text based on patterns, not actually reasoning across unrelated physical domains.

2. AGI: Artificial General Intelligence (or "Strong AI")

Definition: A hypothetical AI system that possesses the ability to understand, learn, and apply intelligence across a broad range of tasks at a level equal to a typical human being.

Capability Level: It features cognitive flexibility. An AGI could learn to play a new video game, write a poem, solve a physics equation, and debate philosophy, transferring knowledge from one domain to another just like a human does.

Current Status: Theoretical / In Development. We do not have AGI yet, though achieving it is the stated goal of major AI labs like OpenAI and Google DeepMind.

3. ASI: Artificial Superintelligence

Definition: An intellect that is vastly smarter than the best human brains in practically every field, including scientific creativity, general wisdom, and social skills.

Capability Level: Far surpasses human cognitive capacity. ASI is often theorized to be achieved shortly after AGI, due to a concept called the "intelligence explosion" (where an AGI system becomes capable of upgrading its own code, rapidly improving its intelligence at a pace humans cannot match).

Current Status: Theoretical / Speculative Future. This stage is the primary focus of AI safety researchers who worry about alignment—ensuring an entity much smarter than humans retains goals aligned with human survival and flourishing.

AI Taxonomy

The diagram above provides a visual taxonomy of the Artificial Intelligence (AI) landscape. It is structured to show two main concepts: the hierarchical evolution of AI algorithms (the nested circles in the center) and the interdisciplinary application domains (the overlapping shapes on the periphery).

  • Symbolic AI: Notice that "Symbolic AI" is written inside the AI circle but outside the nested circles. This is a crucial academic distinction.

    Symbolic AI (or "Rule-based AI" or "Good Old-Fashioned AI") uses explicit human-programmed rules and logic, rather than statistical patterns, to solve problems. It relies on a "knowledge base" (Encoded through symbols, knowledge graphs) and an "inference engine" to deduce answers through If/Then statements, making its decision-making entirely transparent and explainable.

    Early chess programs, for instance, only involved hardcoded rules crafted by programmers, and didn't qualify as machine learning. For a fairly long time, many experts believed that human-level artificial intelligence could be achieved by having programmers handcraft a sufficiently large set of explicit rules for manipulating knowledge. It was the dominant paradigm in AI from the 1950s to the late 1980s. It reached its peak popularity during the expert systems boom of the 1980s.

    Although symbolic AI worked well for structured tasks like chess, it struggled with complex problems such as image recognition, speech processing, and language translation. This limitation led to the rise of machine learning as a more flexible, data-driven approach to AI.

  • The difficulties faced by systems relying on hard-coded knowledge suggest that AI systems need the ability to acquire their own knowledge, by extracting patterns from raw data. This capability is known as machine learning (ML).

    ML is a subset of AI in which systems learn patterns from data rather than following explicitly programmed rules.

    A simple machine learning algorithm called logistic regression can determine whether a tumor is carcinoma or non-carcinoma based on medical data. A simple machine learning algorithm called naive Bayes can separate legitimate e-mail from spam e-mail.

    The performance of these simple machine learning algorithms depends heavily on the representation of the data they are given. For example, when logistic regression is used to classify tumors, the AI system does not examine the patient directly. Instead, the doctor provides the system with several relevant pieces of information, such as tumor size, cell shape, or tissue characteristics. Each piece of information included in the representation of the patient is known as a feature. Logistic regression learns how each of these features correlates with different diagnostic outcomes.

  • Sometimes, it can be very difficult to extract features from raw data (for example, the individual pixels in an image of a red car might be very close to black at night). Neural Network (NN) can solve this problem by learning features on its own.

    NNs are a subset of ML inspired by the structure of the human brain — interconnected nodes that process information in layers. They serve as the architectural backbone for deep learning.

  • Deep Learning (DL) refers to neural networks with many hidden layers, capable of learning highly complex representations. It is a narrower but more powerful subset of NN.
  • Generative AI is a subset of Deep Learning designed to create new, original content—such as text, images, audio, and code—rather than just analyzing or categorizing existing data.
  • Large Language Model (LLM) is a specific type of Generative AI designed to process and produce human-like text. LLMs are built on deep neural networks—specifically, a structure called a Transformer.

Another important area in the diagram is NLP, or Natural Language Processing. NLP focuses on enabling computers to understand, interpret, and produce human language. Historically, NLP included symbolic and rule-based methods, but modern NLP increasingly depends on machine learning and deep learning techniques. This explains why the NLP circle overlaps with both the broader AI space and the learning-based areas. The adjacent "Linguistic" region emphasizes that language technologies are interdisciplinary and connected not only to computer science but also to linguistics, semantics, and cognitive science.

The diagram also highlights application-oriented domains such as Computer Vision & Image Processing and Smart Robots & Sensors. Computer vision focuses on enabling machines to interpret visual information from images and videos, supporting applications such as facial recognition, medical imaging, and object detection. Smart robots and sensor systems combine AI with physical devices and environmental sensing, allowing machines to interact autonomously with the real world in contexts such as robotics, manufacturing, and autonomous vehicles.

Classification of Machine Learning

Supervised Learning:

In supervised learning, the user provides the algorithm with pairs of inputs and desired outputs, and the algorithm finds a way to produce the desired output given an input. In particular, the algorithm is able to create an output for an input it has never seen before without any help from a human.

Machine learning algorithms that learn from input/output pairs are called supervised learning algorithms because a "teacher" provides supervision to the algorithms in the form of the desired outputs for each example that they learn from. While creating a dataset of inputs and outputs is often a laborious manual process, supervised learning algorithms are well understood and their performance is easy to measure.

Supervised learning problems can be further divided into two parts, namely regression and classification.

Regression:

In regression problems we try to predict continuous valued output. That means the output variable is a real value. For example: Given the size of the house, predict the price (real value).

Classification:

A classification problem is when the output variable is a category or a group. That means in classification problems we are trying to predict a discrete number of values, such as "black" or "white" or "spam" and "no spam".

The labels(y) generally come in categorical form and represent a finite number of classes.

Example of some classification tasks:

  1. Given a set of input features, predict whether a Breast Cancer is Benign or Malignant.
  2. Given an image correctly, classify as containing Cats or Dogs.
  3. From a given email, predict whether it's a spam email or not.

Types of classification:

  1. Binary classification — when there is only two classes to predict, usually 1 or 0 values.
  2. Multi-Class Classification — When there are more than two class labels to predict we call multi-classification task, e.g., image classification problems where there are more than thousands classes (cat, dog, fish, car etc.).

Unsupervised Learning

In unsupervised learning, the algorithms are left to themselves to discover interesting structures in the data.

Mathematically, unsupervised learning is when we only have input data (X) and no corresponding output variables.

This is called unsupervised learning because unlike supervised learning above, there are no given correct answers and the machine itself finds the answers.

Unsupervised learning problems can be further divided into association and clustering problems.

Association: An association rule learning problem is where you want to discover rules that describe large portions of your data, such as "people that buy X also tend to buy Y".

Clustering: A clustering problem is where you want to discover the inherent groupings in the data, such as grouping customers by purchasing behaviour.

Reinforcement Learning

The idea behind Reinforcement Learning is that an agent (an AI) will learn from the environment by interacting with it (through trial and error) and receiving rewards (negative or positive) as feedback for performing actions.

The main characters of RL are the agent and the environment. The environment is the world that the agent lives in and interacts with. At every step of interaction, the agent sees a (possibly partial) observation of the state of the world, and then decides on an action to take. The environment changes when the agent acts on it, but may also change on its own.

The agent also perceives a reward signal from the environment, a number that tells it how good or bad the current world state is. The goal of the agent is to maximize its cumulative reward, called return. Reinforcement learning methods are ways that the agent can learn behaviors to achieve its goal.

Neuro-symbolic AI

Neuro-symbolic AI is a combination of two artificial intelligence paradigms, neural networks and symbolic AI, to enable human-like reasoning capabilities. Neural networks excel at pattern recognition, while symbolic AI excels at logic, language, and rule-based reasoning, much like human comprehension. Symbolic AI is also explainable and hence trustworthy. By combining these methods, AI can learn from data while applying logical reasoning to interpret and explain conclusions.

For example, neuro-symbolic AI might identify objects in an image using pattern recognition from neural network algorithm components, then use symbolic logic to infer relationships between them. This enables deeper understanding and reasoning for applications, such as natural language processing, complex problem-solving, and decision-making.

Self driving cars (Tesla and similar autonomous systems) use Neuro-symbolic AI.
